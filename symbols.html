π

Chapter 1

Pi — The Circle's Secret

≈ 3.14159… the number that never ends

Imagine you find a perfectly round plate. You want to know — how long is the edge of this plate? And how does that compare to how wide it is?

So you take a thread, wrap it around the plate's edge, then stretch that thread straight. Surprise! That thread is always exactly 3.14 times the width of the plate. Always. For every circle in the universe. A coin, a pizza, the sun, a tiny button — all of them, same ratio.

The secret in plain words

π = how many times the width fits into the edge.
Wrap a thread around any circle. Straighten it. Divide by the width. You always get 3.14159…

Ancient people discovered this thousands of years ago. They noticed it was always the same mysterious number — never exactly 3, never exactly 3.2 — and its decimal digits go on forever, never repeating. So they gave it a special name from the Greek alphabet: π (pi).

Real life example

Your bicycle tyre has a width (diameter) of 60 cm.
How far do you travel in one full rotation of the wheel?

Distance = π × 60 = 3.14159 × 60 ≈ 188 cm

One spin of the wheel carries you about 188 cm — almost 2 metres. That's π doing its job.

💡 People have memorised π to over 100,000 decimal places as a hobby. The digits go on forever — 3.14159265358979… — and no pattern ever repeats. It's a little bit magic.
e

Chapter 2

e — The Superpower of Growth

≈ 2.71828… the number hiding inside every living thing

Imagine you have ₹100 in a bank. The bank gives you 100% interest per year. At the end of the year — great, you have ₹200. Simple.

Now what if the bank gives you 50% every 6 months instead? You'd have more than ₹200 because the interest is growing on itself too. What if they gave interest every day? Every second? Every single tiny moment?

When you keep splitting the interest into tinier and tinier chunks — approaching every instant — the answer doesn't shoot to infinity. It settles perfectly at one special number:

e in plain words

e ≈ 2.71828…
It is what ₹1 grows to when interest is compounded every single instant for one year at 100%. Nature uses this number to grow everything — bacteria, populations, radioactive decay, even your savings.

A single bacterium becomes 2 after an hour, 4 after two hours, 8 after three… This doubling growth is powered by e. The formula for any growing thing — cells, epidemics, investments, star explosions — almost always has e hiding somewhere inside it.

Zombie apocalypse analogy

One zombie appears in a city. Each zombie creates one new zombie every hour continuously. After just 3 hours:

Zombies = e³ ≈ 20 zombies

That's the power of e. Continuous, relentless, exponential growth. Replace "zombie" with "bacteria" or "rupees" — same formula.

🌱 Snail shells spiral, flowers arrange their petals, and populations explode — all following patterns rooted in e. It is, perhaps, the most naturally occurring number in the universe.
log

Chapter 3

Logarithm — The "How Many Times?" Question

The undo button for multiplication

You know multiplication. 10 × 10 = 100. 10 × 10 × 10 = 1000. But what if someone gives you 1,000,000 and asks — "how many times did someone multiply 10 by itself to get here?"

You already know the answer — 6 times. That's exactly what log does. It asks: how many times was this number multiplied by itself?

log in plain words

log₁₀(1000) = 3 means "10 was multiplied by itself 3 times to make 1000."

Think of it as the reverse of a power. If powers are stairs going up, log is counting how many stairs you climbed.

Why is this useful? Because the real world often grows in multiplications, not additions. An earthquake 100× stronger than another? Richter scale uses logs. Sound 1000× louder? Decibels use logs. The human ear itself perceives loudness on a log scale — that's why a whisper and a shout feel "not that different" even though the sound energy is astronomically different.

The Richter scale story

A magnitude 5 earthquake is not "1 more" than magnitude 4.

Magnitude 5 = 10× more powerful than Magnitude 4

Magnitude 6 = 100× more. Magnitude 7 = 1000× more. Log compresses these enormous differences into small, human-readable numbers.

👂 Your brain is a log calculator. When music goes from 1× to 10× louder, you don't feel it as "10 times louder" — you feel it as "a little bit louder." Evolution built logarithms right into your senses.
ln

Chapter 4

ln — The Natural Log, Log's Cooler Sibling

log with e as the base — growth's own reverse gear

Remember our friend e? The number of continuous growth? Well, ln (the natural logarithm) is simply the log that uses e as its base instead of 10.

If log asks "how many times was 10 multiplied?", ln asks "how many times was e multiplied?" And since e is the number behind all natural growth, ln is the number behind all natural reversal of growth.

ln in plain words

ln(x) = "How long did it take for continuous growth to reach x, starting from 1?"

If e is the accelerator pedal, ln is the odometer — it tells you how far you've come.

Carbon dating — real world superpower

Scientists use ln to figure out how old ancient bones are. Radioactive carbon decays naturally (following e), and ln reverses that to calculate time:

Age = ln(remaining carbon / original) ÷ decay rate

They found that a pharaoh's mummy has 60% of its original carbon. ln tells us exactly how many years ago it was alive. That's ln solving a 3,000-year-old mystery.

⚛️ ln is the most "natural" of all logarithms. It appears in physics, biology, economics, and information theory. Wherever e shows up — and that's everywhere — ln is right around the corner.
Σ

Chapter 5

Sigma — The Lazy Mathematician's Best Friend

Because nobody wants to write "add everything" fifty times

Imagine you're a teacher and you want to add up the marks of 50 students. You could write:

marks₁ + marks₂ + marks₃ + marks₄ + … + marks₅₀

That's exhausting just to write. So mathematicians invented a shorthand. They took the Greek letter Σ (Sigma) — which looks like a big S, standing for "Sum" — and turned it into a command:

Σ in plain words

Σ means "add up all of these."

The numbers on top and bottom tell you where to start and stop. The expression after it tells you what to add. It's like a "for loop" in coding — a compact instruction to repeat something many times.

What Σ actually says

Instead of 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5, a mathematician writes:

Σ (from i=1 to 5) of i

Translation: "Start at 1, go to 5, add each number along the way." Result: 15.

In machine learning, when AI calculates your recommendation feed — it adds up weighted scores of hundreds of videos. That entire process is one big Σ.

Every average you've ever calculated — your CGPA, a cricket batting average, a movie rating — uses Σ behind the scenes. It's the most quietly used symbol in all of mathematics.

💻 Every neural network — the technology inside ChatGPT, image recognition, voice assistants — is essentially a chain of Σ operations happening millions of times per second. Σ is the heartbeat of modern AI.
∫

Chapter 6

Integral — Counting the Uncountable

Like Σ but for infinitely thin slices

You know how to find the area of a rectangle — length × width. Easy. But what's the area of a curved shape? Like the area under a curvy hill? There are no straight edges to multiply.

Here's a clever trick: slice the curved shape into thousands of super-thin rectangles. Each is so thin it's nearly a line. Find the area of each tiny rectangle. Add them all up. The more slices you use, the more accurate your answer gets.

The ∫ (integral) symbol is Σ taken to the extreme — it's "add up infinitely many, infinitely thin slices." The symbol itself is literally a stretched-out S, for "Sum."

∫ in plain words

∫ means "slice into infinite tiny pieces and add them all up."

If Σ is adding 50 student marks, ∫ is adding up every single point in a continuous curve — infinitely many of them.

How your Uber knows the fare

Your Uber doesn't just measure straight-line distance. Your car curves, turns, slows down, speeds up. The app calculates the total distance by slicing your journey into thousands of tiny segments and adding them up:

Total distance = ∫ (speed at each tiny moment × that tiny moment of time)

Every GPS navigation, every animation in a video game, every prediction of where a rocket lands — all powered by integration.

🚀 When NASA calculates a rocket's trajectory to Mars, they're solving enormous integrals — adding up millions of tiny forces (gravity, thrust, solar pressure) acting on the rocket over the course of months. ∫ sends things to space.
√

Chapter 7

Square Root — Finding the Original

If squaring is the question, √ is the answer

You have a square room with an area of 25 square metres. What is the length of one wall? That's it. That's the square root. √25 = 5. Simple as that.

√ (read as "root") asks: what number, when multiplied by itself, gives me this? It's the undo button for squaring. If squaring is "fold the number onto itself," the root is "unfold it back."

√ in plain words

√9 = 3 because 3 × 3 = 9.
√100 = 10 because 10 × 10 = 100.

Think of it as finding the side of a square, if you already know its area.

The WhatsApp group photo problem

You want to arrange 36 friends in a perfect square photo grid. How many columns and rows?

√36 = 6 rows × 6 columns = 36 friends ✓

Engineers use this to split workloads evenly, designers use it to build perfect grids, architects use it to plan tiling — √ is quietly everywhere.

∞

Chapter 8

Infinity — Not a Number, a Direction

The concept that broke and rebuilt mathematics

Here's a question a child once asked a mathematician: "What's the biggest number?" The mathematician smiled and said, "There isn't one." Add 1 to any number, and you get a bigger number. Always. Forever.

∞ (infinity) is not a number you can reach or touch. It's a direction. Like "north" — you can keep walking north forever, but "north" is not a place you arrive at. ∞ is the mathematical way of saying "keeps going, without stopping, forever."

∞ in plain words

∞ is a concept, not a quantity.

You can't add ∞ + 1 and get "infinity plus one." That's why ∞ is written separately — it behaves by completely different rules than normal numbers.

The hotel with infinite rooms

Imagine a hotel with infinite rooms, all occupied. A new guest arrives. Can they get a room?

Yes! Move every guest from room N to room N+1. Room 1 is now free. This thought experiment — Hilbert's Hotel — shows that infinity + 1 = infinity. Infinity is wonderfully, maddingly weird.

♾️ There are different sizes of infinity. The infinity of whole numbers (1, 2, 3…) is actually smaller than the infinity of all decimal numbers between 0 and 1. Georg Cantor proved this in 1891. Infinity is not simple.
· · · · ·

You just met the whole gang.

π lives in every circle. e lives in every growing thing. log and ln reverse that growth. Σ adds everything up. ∫ adds up the infinitely tiny. √ unravels squares. And ∞ reminds us that maths is bigger than any answer we can write down.

Maths was never meant to be scary. It was meant to be wonder, written in shorthand.

Why does the thread-around-the-plate ratio always come out to 3.14159…? Because all circles are the same shape. A tiny coin and the orbit of Earth are both circles — just scaled. When you scale a circle, the circumference and diameter both scale by the same factor. Their ratio is permanently locked.

The unchangeable contract of circles: For any circle, anywhere in the universe — circumference ÷ diameter = π. It cannot be otherwise. It's not a measurement. It's a geometrical truth. Drag the slider and watch it stay exactly 3.14159… regardless of size.

π appears in places with no circles in sight. The sum 1 + 1/4 + 1/9 + 1/16 + 1/25 + … (one over every perfect square) adds up to exactly π²/6. Why is π hiding in number theory?

Because circles are secretly the same as everything periodic. Sound waves, light, alternating current — all of these are circles unrolled through time. π is really the constant of oscillation. Circles are just the most visible place it lives. This is why π appears in quantum mechanics, signal processing, and probability distributions that look nothing like circles.

Here is the most beautiful equation in all of mathematics. It was discovered by Euler in the 18th century and has been called "the most remarkable formula in mathematics" ever since:

eiπ + 1 = 0
Euler's Identity unites five fundamental constants: e (growth), i (imaginary unit, √−1), π (circles), 1 (the unit), and 0 (nothing). It says: rotate by π radians on the complex plane and add 1 — you arrive at zero. Mathematics is not a random collection of facts. It is one single, breathtaking, interconnected story.

The extraordinary thing about eˣ in calculus: its slope at every point equals its own height. It is the only function with this property.

d/dx(eˣ) = eˣ — the rate of growth equals the amount.

This is why real growth in nature follows e. Bacteria, viral spread, compound interest — whenever growth is proportional to current size, e emerges automatically. eˣ is the unique solution to the equation "I grow at the same rate as I am tall."

e connects to probability in a stunning way. Shuffle a deck of cards completely randomly. What is the chance that no card ends up in its original position? As the deck grows:

Probability → 1/e ≈ 36.79%… exactly.

It doesn't matter if it's 10 cards or 10 million. The answer stabilises at 1/e. e is woven into the fabric of randomness itself.

Also: e = 1/0! + 1/1! + 1/2! + 1/3! + 1/4! + … — an infinite sum of fractions of factorials that adds up precisely to e. The most natural number in the universe, arriving from the most unexpected directions.

log can have any base. log₁₀(1000)=3 because 10³=1000. log₂(8)=3 because 2³=8. The base tells you "what number am I multiplying?" The result tells you "how many times?"

Three flavours: log₁₀ used in science (Richter, decibels, pH) · log₂ used in computing (binary, algorithm complexity) · ln used in calculus (because of e). When you see plain "log" with no base, it's usually log₁₀ in science or log₂ in CS. Context is everything.

The superpower: log turns multiplication into addition. This single property ran the entire financial and scientific world for 400 years before calculators.

log(A × B) = log(A) + log(B)

To multiply two 7-digit numbers, you'd look up their logs in a table, add two small numbers, then reverse-lookup. Addition is trivially easy; multiplication is tedious and error-prone. The slide rule — a physical logarithm calculator — is what sent Apollo 11 to the moon. Logarithms are the original computing shortcut.

log₂ is the heart of computer science. Every time an algorithm halves its search space, it's using log₂ unknowingly:

Binary search in log₂(n) steps
1,000 contacts → max 10 steps
1,000,000 contacts → max 20 steps
1,000,000,000 contacts → max 30 steps

The data size grew a million times over. The steps only grew by 20. That's logarithmic efficiency — and it's why Google searches the entire internet in milliseconds. Every "O(log n)" rating in software engineering is secretly a thank-you note to logarithms.

ln(x) asks: "how long does continuous growth take to reach x, starting from 1?" It's the reverse gear of eˣ. eˣ takes time and gives quantity. ln takes quantity and gives time.

e^(ln x) = x  and  ln(e¹) = 1

They are exact opposites. Wherever e multiplies, ln divides back. Like squaring and √ undoing each other — these two are inextricably linked. If you know one, you know the other.

The geometric definition: ln(x) is literally the area under the curve y = 1/t from t=1 to t=x. The slider shows this directly — watch the shaded area equal ln(x) as you drag.

ln(x) = ∫₁ˣ (1/t) dt

This was the original definition, found centuries before anyone linked it to e. Mathematicians computing areas under 1/x kept finding the same mysterious function. Much later they realised it was e's inverse — and the mathematical community was stunned.

Claude Shannon's entropy formula — the foundation of all information theory, data compression, and cryptography — uses ln:

H = −Σ p · ln(p)
This says: the information content of a message equals the negative log of its probability. An unlikely surprise has high −ln(p) — lots of information. An expected "yes" has p≈1, so −ln(1)=0 — zero information.

Σ is a compact instruction: "loop through these values and add them all." The Gauss story: asked to sum 1+2+…+100, nine-year-old Gauss saw that 1+100=101, 2+99=101… 50 such pairs. Answer: 50×101=5050. Instantly.

Gauss's formula: Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ i = n(n+1)/2

Instead of adding n numbers, do one multiplication. For n=15: sum = 15×16/2 = 120. The formula is derived from the same pairing trick. Press "Add next number" below — the Gauss formula column always matches the running sum column. That's the magic.

Some infinite sums converge (settle at a finite number). Some don't. This distinction is one of the deepest ideas in mathematics.

Convergent (finite sum): 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + … = 1 exactly · 1 + 1/4 + 1/9 + 1/16 + … = π²/6

Divergent (goes to ∞): 1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/4 + … = ∞ (harmonic series)

Fourier's revelation: any repeating signal — sound, light, temperature cycles — can be written as Σ of simple sine waves. This is the Fourier series.

Square wave = Σ (4/πk) × sin(kx) for odd k = 1, 3, 5, 7…

A harsh, angular, digital square wave is secretly just infinitely many smooth sine waves added together. Decompress MP3 audio, JPEGs, and WiFi signals with Fourier waves!

∫ is a stretched S, standing for Sum. Leibniz invented the notation in 1675. The idea: take Σ (sum of finite rectangles), make the rectangles infinitely thin (dx), and you have ∫. The slider shows exactly this transition.

∫ₐᵇ f(x)dx = "Slice the area under f(x) from a to b into infinitely thin rectangles of height f(x) and width dx. Add them all."

More slices = thinner rectangles = more accurate area. At infinity slices: exact. Below, watch as you increase the number of rectangles and the area converges to the true value of 9.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus — perhaps the most important theorem in all of mathematics — says integration and differentiation are exact opposites:

d/dx [∫ₐˣ f(t)dt] = f(x)

Newton and Leibniz discovered this independently — sparking one of history's most bitter intellectual rivalry disputes, running for decades.

In probability, every calculation involving a continuous variable (height, temperature, time) is secretly an integral:

P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = ∫ₐᵇ f(x)dx

The probability that someone is between 170–175cm is the area under the normal bell curve between those values. All probability for continuous variables lives in intervals, computed by ∫.

√ means "find the side of a square given its area." But its real power is in the Pythagorean theorem: c = √(a²+b²). Every distance calculation in the universe uses this.

GPS uses √ 10 million times a second to compute your position from satellite distances. Google Maps uses it for every route segment. Every physics engine in every video game uses it to check distances between objects.

The discovery of √2 nearly destroyed Greek mathematics. The Pythagoreans believed all numbers were ratios of whole numbers (fractions). Then someone asked: what is the diagonal of a unit square?

√2 = 1.41421356… (never ends, never repeats)

The irrational numbers shattered the idea that all of mathematics could be built from counting numbers alone. Mathematics grew richer!

What is √(−1)? No real number squared gives a negative. Rather than declaring "impossible," mathematicians invented a new number:

i = √(−1), and i² = −1

This opened an entirely new dimension — the complex plane — where numbers have both real and imaginary parts. What seemed like a mathematical fantasy became the language of quantum mechanics.

∞ is not a destination. It's a direction — like north. You can keep heading north forever, but "north" is not a place. In calculus we never divide by ∞ directly; we ask what happens as numbers approach it.

lim(x→∞) 1/x = 0

As x grows without stopping, 1/x shrinks toward zero without ever quite reaching it. This is calculus's relationship with infinity. Press "Add term" below to see Zeno's paradox in action.

Georg Cantor proved in 1891 that there are different sizes of infinity. The counting numbers (1, 2, 3…) are a smaller infinity than the real numbers between 0 and 1.

The diagonal argument (simplified): Reals are uncountably infinite. Georg Cantor called these different infinite levels ℵ₀ (aleph-null) and ℵ₁ (aleph-one).

The most mind-bending result: what is 0.9999… (nines repeating forever)?

0.999… = 1. Exactly. Not "almost 1." Equal to 1.

Proof: x = 0.999… → 10x = 9.999… → 10x − x = 9 → 9x = 9 → x = 1. The number line is not a row of discrete beads — it's an unbroken continuum.
