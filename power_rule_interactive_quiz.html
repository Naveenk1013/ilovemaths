Core formula
d/dx [xn] = n·xn−1
f(x) = xⁿ → f′(x) = n·xⁿ⁻¹
Bring the exponent down as a coefficient, then subtract 1 from the exponent. Works for any real n.

Memorisation tricks, common traps, and mental models that will make the power rule second nature — fast.

🧠 Memorisation tricks
The "Bring Down, Knock Down" mantra
Chant this every time: bring the exponent down in front, knock the exponent down by one. Two moves, always in that order. For x⁵ — bring 5 down → 5x, knock exponent down → 5x⁴. Done.
The "Stairs" visual
Picture the exponent climbing down one stair to become the coefficient. x³ → 3x² — the 3 steps down from the roof to the front door, and the power is now one floor lower. It literally descends.
Finger trick for quick mental math
Hold up fingers equal to n. That's your new coefficient. Then count one less — that's your new exponent. For x⁶: hold 6 fingers → coefficient is 6, count one less → 6x⁵. Silly but it sticks.
⚠️ Common mistakes to avoid
Forgetting to multiply the existing coefficient
The most frequent error. d/dx[4x³] ≠ 3x². You must multiply: 4 × 3 = 12, giving 12x². Every time there's a number in front of x, multiply it by n.
Not rewriting radicals and fractions first
You cannot apply the rule directly to √x or 1/x². Rewrite first: √x = x^½, then differentiate. 1/x² = x⁻², then differentiate. Rewrite → Apply → Simplify.
Thinking constants differentiate to themselves
A flat line has zero slope. d/dx[8] = 0, always. Constants vanish. This surprises beginners — remember, a constant has no rate of change, ever.
Forgetting the sign on negative exponents
d/dx[x⁻³] = −3x⁻⁴ — the negative n becomes the coefficient, so the result is negative. Don't drop the minus sign when bringing the exponent down.
⚡ Speed tricks for exams
🔁
Term-by-term shortcut for polynomials
For x³ + 5x² − 2x + 7, hit each term separately in one pass: 3x² + 10x − 2 + 0. Constants disappear automatically — don't even write them in your working.
🔢
n × (n−1) pattern for x² and x³
Derivatives of powers form a memorable sequence: x → 1, x² → 2x, x³ → 3x², x⁴ → 4x³. The coefficient and exponent always differ by 1 in this sequence. Memorise the first four and the rest follows.
↕️
Fraction shortcut: flip and differentiate
For 1/xⁿ, the answer is always −n/x^(n+1). The sign flips negative, the denominator exponent goes up by 1. 1/x⁴ → −4/x⁵. Much faster than converting to negative exponent form.
✅
Self-check: degree always drops by 1
If your original function has degree n, the derivative must have degree n−1. Differentiate a cubic (degree 3)? Expect a quadratic (degree 2). If not — you made an error. Takes 1 second to verify.
