Power Rule

The first and most used rule of differentiation. Master it here.

$$ \frac{d}{dx} [x^n] = n x^{n-1} $$
$$ f(x) = x^n \implies f'(x) = n x^{n-1} $$
Bring the exponent down as a coefficient, then subtract 1 from the exponent. Works for any real n.
3-step method
01
Identify n
Spot the exponent on x. That number is n.
02
Multiply by n
Write n in front as a coefficient (× any existing coefficient).
03
Decrease n by 1
The new exponent is n − 1. That's your derivative.
All cases you will encounter
Positive integer Standard case
$$ \frac{d}{dx} [x^n] = n x^{n-1} $$
x⁵→5x⁴  |  x³→3x²
With coefficient Constant multiple
$$ \frac{d}{dx} [c x^n] = c n x^{n-1} $$
4x³→12x²  |  7x²→14x
n = 0 Constant → zero
$$ \frac{d}{dx} [c] = 0 $$
$$ \frac{d}{dx}[5] = 0 $$  |  $$ \frac{d}{dx}[\pi] = 0 $$
Negative exponent Fractions / 1/xⁿ
$$ \frac{d}{dx} [x^{-n}] = -n x^{-n-1} $$
1/x = x⁻¹→−x⁻²
n = 1 (linear) Slope of x
$$ \frac{d}{dx} [x] = 1 $$
3x→3  |  −2x→−2
Fractional exponent Roots / radicals
$$ \frac{d}{dx} \left[ x^{p/q} \right] = \frac{p}{q} x^{\frac{p}{q} - 1} $$
√x→½x^(−½)

Memorisation tricks, common traps, and mental models that will make the power rule second nature — fast.

Memorisation tricks
The "Bring Down, Knock Down" mantra
Chant this every time: bring the exponent down in front, knock the exponent down by one. Two moves, always in that order. For x⁵ — bring 5 down → 5x, knock exponent down → 5x⁴. Done.
The "Stairs" visual
Picture the exponent climbing down one stair to become the coefficient. x³ → 3x² — the 3 steps down from the roof to the front door, and the power is now one floor lower.
Finger trick for quick mental math
Hold up fingers equal to n. That's your new coefficient. Then count one less — that's your new exponent. For x⁶: hold 6 fingers → coefficient is 6, count one less → 6x⁵.
Common mistakes to avoid
Forgetting to multiply the existing coefficient
d/dx[4x³] ≠ 3x². You must multiply: 4 × 3 = 12, giving 12x². Every time there's a number in front of x, multiply it by n.
Not rewriting radicals and fractions first
You cannot apply the rule directly to √x or 1/x². Rewrite first: √x = x^½, then differentiate. 1/x² = x⁻², then differentiate.
Thinking constants differentiate to themselves
A flat line has zero slope. d/dx[8] = 0, always. Constants vanish — they have no rate of change.
Forgetting the sign on negative exponents
d/dx[x⁻³] = −3x⁻⁴ — the negative n becomes the coefficient, so the result is negative. Don't drop the minus sign.
Speed tricks for exams
→
Term-by-term shortcut for polynomials
For x³ + 5x² − 2x + 7, hit each term separately: 3x² + 10x − 2 + 0. Constants disappear automatically.
#
n × (n−1) pattern for x² and x³
Derivatives form a memorable sequence: x → 1, x² → 2x, x³ → 3x², x⁴ → 4x³. Memorise the first four and the rest follows.
↕
Fraction shortcut: flip and differentiate
For 1/xⁿ, the answer is always −n/x^(n+1). 1/x⁴ → −4/x⁵. Much faster than converting.
✓
Self-check: degree always drops by 1
Differentiate a cubic (degree 3)? Expect a quadratic (degree 2). If not — you made an error.
