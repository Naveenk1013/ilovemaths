Permutations & Combinations

Understand when order matters and when it doesn't. Master factorials, nPr, and nCr formulas.

Does Order Matter?

Choosing a president and a vice-president is different from choosing two equal committee members. If order matters, it's a Permutation. If order doesn't matter, it's a Combination.

$$ ^nC_r = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} $$
The Combination formula. Divide by $r!$ to remove duplicate arrangements!
ConceptFactorials ($n!$)

Multiply a number by all integers below it.

$$ 4! = 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 24 $$
Note: $$ 0! = 1 $$
Order MattersPermutations ($^nP_r$)

Choosing $r$ objects from $n$, where sequence matters.

$$ ^nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!} $$
Ex: Passwords, race podiums.
Order FreeCombinations ($^nC_r$)

Choosing $r$ objects from $n$, where sequence DOES NOT matter.

$$ ^nC_r = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} $$
Ex: Fruit salad, card hands.

What is the relation between Permutation ($^nP_r$) and Combination ($^nC_r$)?
Step 1
Analyze the given options:
  • Option A: $ ^nP_r = r! \cdot ^nC_r $
  • Option B: $ ^nC_r = r! \cdot ^nP_r $
  • Option C: $ ^nP_r = ^nC_r $
  • Option D: $ ^nP_r = \frac{^nC_r}{r!} $
Step 2
$\text{Apply Core Principles}$
Permutations account for the arrangement of selected items ($r!$). Thus, the number of permutations is $r!$ times the combinations: $ ^nP_r = r! \cdot ^nC_r $.
Step 3
Cross-reference choices to select the valid deduction. Option A perfectly aligns with the principles analyzed.
Final Answer: Option A ($ ^nP_r = r! \cdot ^nC_r $)
