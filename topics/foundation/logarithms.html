Logarithms

Understand how logarithms flip exponents, decode their rules, and simplify complex multiplications into additions.

What is a Logarithm?

A logarithm is simply the opposite of an exponent. If you know that a base raised to a power equals a number, the logarithm tells you what that power is.

$$ y = b^x \iff \log_b(y) = x $$
"Base $b$ raised to the power of $x$ gives $y$." So, the log is asking: "What power do I raise $b$ to, to get $y$?"
Concept Translating to Logs

Every exponent equation can be written as a log equation.

$$ 2^3 = 8 \implies \log_2(8) = 3 $$ $$ 10^2 = 100 \implies \log_{10}(100) = 2 $$
Law 1 Product Rule

Log of a multiplication becomes addition of logs.

$$ \log_b(xy) = \log_b(x) + \log_b(y) $$
$$ \log(5 \times 2) = \log(5) + \log(2) $$
Law 2 Quotient Rule

Log of a division becomes subtraction of logs.

$$ \log_b\left(\frac{x}{y}\right) = \log_b(x) - \log_b(y) $$
$$ \log\left(\frac{10}{2}\right) = \log(10) - \log(2) $$
Law 3 Power Rule

An exponent inside a log jumps out to the front as a multiplier.

$$ \log_b(x^n) = n \cdot \log_b(x) $$
$$ \log(x^3) = 3 \log(x) $$
Notation Common vs Natural Log

Special shorthand for base 10 and base $e$.

$$ \log(x) \text{ means } \log_{10}(x) $$ $$ \ln(x) \text{ means } \log_e(x) $$
Quick Revision Box
What is $\log_b(1)$?$$ 0 $$ (because $b^0 = 1$)
What is $\log_b(b)$?$$ 1 $$ (because $b^1 = b$)
Can $x$ in $\log(x)$ be negative?No! You can't take the log of a negative number or zero.

✓ Logarithms downgrade operations
Think of logs as downgrading mathematics by one level of difficulty:
  • Powers downgrade to Multiplication (Power Rule)
  • Multiplication downgrades to Addition (Product Rule)
  • Division downgrades to Subtraction (Quotient Rule)
# Common Mistake
$$ \log(x + y) $$ DOES NOT equal $$ \log(x) + \log(y) $$. There is no rule to expand a sum inside a logarithm!
→ Change of Base Formula
If your calculator only has $\ln$ and $\log_{10}$, you can calculate any base using: $$ \log_b(a) = \frac{\log(a)}{\log(b)} $$
